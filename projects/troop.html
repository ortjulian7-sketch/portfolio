Summary

TROOP lets everyday investors buy fractional participations in commercial real estate — instead of needing capital for a full property, users invest in a share and earn proportional monthly rental income. I designed and built the entire platform myself, as the only Bubble developer on the team — owning the database architecture, the full UI implementation, and the financial logic behind investment returns, working from direction set by the CTO, CEO, and head of technology.

Description

One developer, the entire product.

TROOP needed to handle multiple interdependent flows: property listings with investment terms, KYC and background-check onboarding for investors, transaction tracking, and — the hardest part — proportional profit distribution logic that calculates and reflects each investor's earnings based on their stake in a property. I built that financial logic from scratch.

I was "the team in one" — the sole Bubble developer responsible for migrating the platform off WordPress and implementing it end-to-end. A UX designer on the team defined the design system; I took it from there, implementing every screen, workflow, and integration myself while the platform had real users and real money flowing through it during the transition.

Beyond the build, I worked daily with the CTO, CEO, and head of technology to translate business requirements into working product — making the calls on how data should be structured and how each flow should behave under real usage.

Problem, solution & results

The problem

WordPress couldn't support the data complexity TROOP needed — multi-user roles, live investment tracking, KYC compliance, and proportional payout logic. The team needed a single developer who could own the rebuild without a large engineering org.

The solution

A full rebuild in Bubble, solo — with a database schema designed around investment relationships from day one, and a stack of integrations (KYC, payments, CRM, automation) wired together to support a real financial product.

The results

A platform that's been running in production for over 3 years, scaling to ~1,400 active users with zero structural rework needed — the architecture has held up as the product grew.
